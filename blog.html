Fix Cafe'

7/13/2017

0 Comments

 

Today we want to showcase one of our retail projects: Fix Cafe'.

Located in Lanterns Mall, in Hastings, Barbados, this cafe' serves Italian gelato, locally roasted delicious coffee and awesome pastry.
Completion date was September 2016 but, my bad, I waited too long to ask Mario Porchetta Photography to help me documenting the end result - thanks Mario!

We decided for an industrial cozy modern look, warmed by the use of plenty of wood and pastel colors.
Small walls are used as dividers and repeated design elements to split the cafe into the different areas for the various products offered: ice cream display, visible from outside, orders placing and cash register, salads, sushi and beverages display, pastry display and coffee station.
All tops are white ash hardwood, counters facing are honey stained pine slats to provide ventilation to all the equipment and displays, stainless steel kitchen and fridges.
Ceilings were left open and painted black for an easy access and maintenance.
Two zinc coated trunking hang from the ceiling, hiding all the electrical and AC conduits and allowing ease of change in the lighting location.
Two double sliding doors dress the end wall and hide a storage and service closet.
Floors are done in epoxy resin with a large wood plank texture: nice, durable and easy to clean.

We also provide a variety of different seats: stools for high tables, colorful chairs for tables and a sofa set for the small "living" area, for longer and more comfortable permanence.

Artwork on the walls, handmade by me, is done using coffee bags burlap and painted apple crates.
They display books and magazines for the customers to use or borrow.
Door's and windows graphics catch the passers by attention and signal the cafe' in the mall.

﻿Biophilic Design

12/7/2016

0 Comments

 
I did not know about Biophilic Design before and, bumpeing into this article, opened new horizons!

​
www.bbc.com/capital/story/20161125-why-you-cant-afford-to-ignore-nature-in-the-workplace?ocid
Biophilia can be defined as a love of life and the living world; the affinity of human beings for otherlife forms.
Apply this to design & architecture and you will discover new ways to say something that have very ancient origins: we all need nature to be part of our life and part of our living environment.

It's interesting to apply this concept to architecture and design in a more scientific way.
Researches tell us that nature within our work and living spaces make us heathier, more productive, less stressed and more creative.
Again, nothing new but I think it's a sign of the direction we should all move towards.

As soon as I've explored the biophilic grounds, I could find a lot more.

TooWheels

10/11/2016

0 Comments

 
TooWheels is a fantastic project.
I want to share it because it is inspirational, because it is Open Source, because it is a social project, because it is cad/cam and... because I like it!

​It is a sport wheelchair that can be build with simple materials and tools: some pipes, some plywood (CAD/CAM cut or do-it-yourself) and some bicycle parts.
Dimensions and size can be changed at will, making it completely custom.

​TooWheels is a project also released in an Open Source version for non-commercial use.
It can be downloaded at toowheels.org/ and built anywhere in the world.
Building it yourself should cost between 180 and 250 euro.

TooWheels has been designed by Fabrizio Alessio, designer and maker based in Turin, Italy.
For this great project he has been awarded, by ADI (Industrial Design Association) the Compasso D'Oro Targa Giovani, the prestigious industrial design award for young designers.

​Well done Fabrizio!

Rethinking social housing

1/6/2016

0 Comments

 
Here's some food for thoughts.

http://www.theguardian.com/cities/2016/jan/04/crime-community-designer-social-housing-winnipeg
Even the best projects, done with the best intentions, can miss the target.
And even the best architects can slip and not do their home works before start designing (ouch!).
This is particularly wrong in the case of social housing.
We should be especially careful and put up our best efforts on social housing projects because this shows what kind of persons and human beings we are, before we are architects.
Too often and too sadly, it's quite the opposite and social housing is made with the minimal money possible, with the highest density standards possible, with the most cement and least care.
The world is full of poorly done social housing projects that, instead of dignifying the people who live there and uplift the whole neighborhoods, only create slums and ghettos.

I think it's time to change attitude and see social housing projects as an occasion to do great things for our fellow citizens instead of a problem to be quickly and cheaply solve.

"Fratino" Dining Table: a CNC machine cut prototype

2/18/2015

1 Comment

 
I've been asked to design a dining table for my good friends P&S last year.
After getting very high quotations on my first design and inspired by blogs and web sites like OpenDesk, I've decided to design a CNC machine cut dining table.
P&S required the table to comfortably seat 10 people (up to 12) for hosting; it should also have the possibility to get smaller for everyday use.

The "Fratino" - literally "little friar" in Italian - is a long and narrow table type,  traditionally used by friars in monasteries.
I have an antique fratino table in my house and the dining experience is nicer and more intimate than standard tables.
I liked the idea of a narrow table so that people can dine close in front to each other; the table type was also ideal to be by-folding to extend its capacity.

The table is 7 ft (2133 mm) long and 2 ft (609 mm) wide when closed - 4 ft (1219 mm) open.

Hope you like it!!!

A big hug to P&S that contributed to this project!
And a special thanks goes to Alan Cox and AC Woodhouse for making this project come true; Randy Seraphin and the workshop guys made an amazing job!

Liquid Orchids

11/19/2013

0 Comments

 
Fabian Oefner unites gravity and paint to create liquid orchids

For his latest series of paint action photography, Swiss artist Fabian Oefner has once again captured ephemeral beauty by uniting gravity and liquid chroma to yield delicate and fragile ‘orchid’ structures.
Through the synthesis of natural forces and acrylic paint, blossom-like arrangements emerge from the fluid matter. Filling a tank with several layers of different tones — with the top coat being either black or white — Oefner drops a sphere directly into the solution.
As the plunging object falls, the paint it splashes into is forced upwards, shaping the blend of liquified hues into floral-like forms. when photographed with high speed cameras, an image is permanently preserved, capturing a moment that transpires for only a fraction of a second before disappearing beneath the surface again.
